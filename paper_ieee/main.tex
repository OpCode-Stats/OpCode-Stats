\begin{abstract}
Compiled binaries, viewed as flat sequences of machine opcodes, exhibit
statistical structure analogous to natural language and biological sequences.
We present \emph{OpCode-Stats}\footnote{\url{https://github.com/OpCode-Stats/OpCode-Stats}},
an open-source toolkit that applies information-theoretic and computational
biology techniques to instruction streams.  Across multiple corpora
(15--40 binaries, 2.9--3.7\,M instructions), three independent validation
sets, and a 100-binary compiler matrix, we measure opcode frequency
distributions, $n$-gram entropy rates, compressibility, motifs, and
manifold dimensionality.  Programs occupy a thin structured manifold
($d_{95}{=}8$ of 200 bigram dimensions) with strongly Zipfian opcode
concentration ($\hat{\alpha}_{\mathrm{MLE}}{\approx}1.4$); a bigram language
model attains perplexity $8.5$, within $0.07$\,bits of the empirical
conditional-entropy lower bound.  Systematic ablation shows the structure
persists after aggressive boilerplate stripping (max $3.2\%$ change),
replicates across corpora, and is compiler-invariant
(compiler $R^2{<}0.03$).
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
binary analysis, opcode statistics, information theory, language models,
compression, Zipf's law
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Source code is parsed, compiled through many transformations, and reduced to
a sequence of machine opcodes that discards all high-level structure.  Yet,
as we show, this opcode stream retains profound statistical regularity.
Bioinformatics offers a natural toolkit for such sequences: $k$-mer analysis,
entropy profiling, motif discovery, and information-theoretic
metrics~\cite{vinga2003alignment}.  We extend this toolkit to compiled
binaries.

\textbf{Contributions.}
(i) An open-source analysis toolkit, \emph{OpCode-Stats}, applying
computational-biology techniques to compiled binaries.
(ii) Empirical characterisation of opcode frequency concentration:
heavy-tailed rank-frequency distribution
(MLE $\hat{\alpha}{\approx}1.4$; KS $0.117$); a unigram--sequential
decomposition shows $69\%$ of compression gain over random arises from
frequency skew, $31\%$ from sequential structure.
(iii) Quantification of $n$-gram entropy and deviation from a
unigram-shuffled baseline (gap $1.12$ bits at $n{=}5$), revealing
multi-scale dependencies.
(iv) A controlled compiler matrix
(5 projects $\times$ \{gcc,clang\} $\times$ \{-O0,-O2,-O3\}) showing
source identity and compiler identity are comparably strong determinants
of similarity (both $p{<}10^{-4}$, $r{\approx}0.5$;
project vs.\ compiler: $p{=}0.38$); compiler variation adds new manifold
dimensions ($d_{95}$ doubles from 8 to 17).
(v) Empirical validation that $n$-gram language model perplexity tracks
the conditional-entropy lower bound (bigram gap ${<}0.07$ bits).

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\textbf{Opcode-level analysis.}
Bilar~\cite{bilar2007opcodes} observed Zipf-like distributions in PE files;
subsequent work used $n$-gram opcode features for malware
classification~\cite{masud2008machine,santos2013opcode}, focused on
discriminative accuracy rather than information structure.  The closest
prior cross-domain study is Gan et al.~\cite{gan2009statistical},
who applied $n$-tuple Zipf analysis to DNA, English, source, and binary.
We extend with mutual-information decay, LZ78 complexity, motif discovery,
clone detection, and a controlled compiler experiment.  In the malware
domain, MAlign~\cite{saha2024malign} encodes raw bytes as nucleotides for
genome-style alignment.  Recent binary embedding systems
(PalmTree~\cite{li2021palmtree}, jTrans~\cite{wang2022jtrans},
HermesSim~\cite{hermessim2024}, Nova~\cite{nova2025}) implicitly rely on
the regularities OpCode-Stats makes explicit; Marcelli et
al.~\cite{marcelli2022machine} provide a benchmark for similarity search.

\textbf{Compression and information theory.}
NCD~\cite{cilibrasi2005clustering} provides a parameter-free similarity
measure rooted in Kolmogorov complexity.  The
\emph{naturalness} hypothesis~\cite{hindle2012naturalness,tu2014localness}
states software is more predictable than natural language; cross-entropy of
token-level LMs on code is lower than on prose.  We extend this to the
binary level.  Motif discovery from genomics~\cite{das2007survey} has
analogues in compiler-idiom recognition~\cite{schulte2018evolving}, but
systematic statistical characterisation is absent from prior literature.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\textbf{Corpora.}  We analyse two corpora of GNU/Linux ELF x86-64 binaries
from \texttt{/usr/bin}: \emph{Smoke} (15 binaries: grep, sed, gzip, bzip2,
xz, find, tar, bash, python3, curl, git, vim, nano, gcc, objdump) and
\emph{Coreutils-system} (40 binaries spanning 11 functional categories,
$\leq 10$\,MB each).  Sequences are extracted via \texttt{objdump -d}
(60\,s timeout per binary); only mnemonics are retained, yielding
$s{=}(o_1,\dots,o_N)$ over a vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$.

\textbf{Frequency and Zipf.}
We fit Zipf's law $f(v)\propto r(v)^{-\alpha}$ via OLS on log-log
coordinates and via MLE on the truncated discrete Zipf model
($\ell(\alpha){=}-\alpha\sum_r f(r)\log r - N\log Z_V(\alpha)$ with
$Z_V(\alpha){=}\sum_{r=1}^V r^{-\alpha}$).  95\% CIs come from
$n{=}200$ bootstrap resamples; goodness-of-fit is reported via the
Kolmogorov--Smirnov statistic against the fitted rank-CDF.

\textbf{Entropy and shuffled baseline.}
$n$-gram entropy $H_n{=}-\sum_w p(w)\log_2 p(w)$ and entropy rate
$\hr{n}{=}H_n/n$ are computed for $n{=}1,\dots,5$.  A unigram-preserving
shuffle baseline isolates higher-order dependencies; a negative
\emph{entropy gain} $\Delta\hr{n}{=}\hr{n}^{\text{real}}-\hr{n}^{\text{shuf}}$
for $n{>}1$ confirms genuine multi-opcode structure.

\textbf{Compression and LZ78.}
We measure compression ratios $\rho{=}|C(s)|/|s|$ under \zlib{} and
\lzma{}, and LZ78 normalised complexity
$\tilde{c}{=}K/\log_2 N$~\cite{ziv1978compression} (where $K$ is the
distinct-phrase count).  Both are compared to a uniform-random baseline
(maximum entropy) and a unigram-shuffled baseline (preserves marginals).
The gap between real and shuffled isolates compression gain attributable
to \emph{sequential} structure beyond Zipfian skew.

\textbf{Motifs, similarity, manifold.}
A $k$-mer motif is a tuple $(o_i,\dots,o_{i+k-1})$; we enumerate motifs
for $k{\in}\{4,\dots,12\}$ and retain those covering ${\geq}1\%$ of
functions.  Pairwise similarity uses NCD
$(C(xy)-\min(C(x),C(y)))/\max(C(x),C(y))$ with \zlib{}/\lzma{}, and
L1-normalised $n$-gram cosine.  Mutual information $I(o_t;o_{t+\ell})$
is estimated for lags $\ell{=}1,\dots,50$.  Corpus manifold
dimensionality $d_{95}$ is the PCA component count explaining $95\%$
of inter-binary variance on top-200 bigram frequency vectors.

\textbf{$n$-gram language models.}
We train Laplace-smoothed $n$-gram LMs ($n{=}1,\dots,5$) jointly on an
80/20 token split.  We report cross-entropy
$H_{\mathrm{LM}}(n){=}-\tfrac{1}{T}\sum_t\log_2 P(o_t|o_{t-n+1:t-1})$,
perplexity $\mathrm{PPL}(n){=}2^{H_{\mathrm{LM}}(n)}$, and the smoothing
gap $\delta(n){=}H_{\mathrm{LM}}(n)-(H_n-H_{n-1})$ vs.\ the empirical
conditional-entropy lower bound.

\textbf{Clone detection.}
A three-stage pipeline: MinHash signatures
($k{\in}\{3,4,5\}$, $m{=}128$ permutations), LSH at Jaccard thresholds
$\tau{\in}\{0.70,0.90\}$, and Smith--Waterman alignment with normalised
score $s{=}S/\min(|f_1|,|f_2|)$.  Pairs are classified Type 1
($J{\geq}0.95$), Type 2 ($J{\geq}0.70$), Type 3 ($J{\geq}0.50$); families
are connected components.

\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Corpus and Vocabulary}

After inode deduplication, the smoke corpus contains 15 binaries
(2.91\,M instructions, $|\mathcal{V}|{=}281$); coreutils-system contains
40 binaries (3.74\,M instructions, $|\mathcal{V}|{=}301$),
spanning 11 functional categories (Table~\ref{tab:corpus}).

The top-5 opcodes (\texttt{mov}, \texttt{lea}, \texttt{call}, \texttt{jmp},
\texttt{push}) account for over $50\%$ of instructions.

\textbf{Zipf's law.}
OLS on log-log coordinates yields $\hat{\alpha}_{\text{OLS}}{=}3.34$
($95\%$ CI $[3.05,3.67]$, $R^2{=}0.92$).  MLE on the truncated discrete
Zipf model yields $\hat{\alpha}_{\text{MLE}}{=}1.44$
($95\%$ CI $[1.438,1.440]$, KS $0.117$).  The two diverge because OLS
overweights the long tail of near-zero-frequency opcodes; MLE,
properly weighted by counts, lies closer to natural-language
$\alpha{\approx}1$.  The KS statistic exceeds the asymptotic critical
value at $n{=}281$ ($0.081$), so a strict truncated power law is
rejected; the distribution is best characterised as heavy-tailed with
approximately Zipfian concentration (Fig.~\ref{fig:zipf}).

\end{figure}

\subsection{$n$-gram Entropy}

The entropy rate of real sequences decreases by $1.12$\,bits from $n{=}1$
($\hr{1}{=}3.98$) to $n{=}5$ ($\hr{5}{=}2.86$); the shuffled baseline
decreases by only $0.30$\,bits, reflecting near-independence after
shuffling.  $\Delta\hr{n}{<}0$ for all $n{>}1$ confirms genuine
multi-instruction dependencies (Fig.~\ref{fig:entropy}).
Per-binary analysis shows shells and compilers exhibit lower entropy than
compression utilities, consistent with predictable
control-flow-heavy code vs.\ data-transform pipelines.

  \label{fig:entropy}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Compression and LZ78}

Real binaries compress to $\rho_{\zlib}{=}0.221$ vs.\ $0.360$ shuffled
and $0.666$ random; \lzma{} gives $0.184/0.303/0.553$
(Table~\ref{tab:compression}).  Decomposing $\Delta{=}0.445$ over random,

vs.\ $45{,}187$ (shuffled) and $87{,}881$ (random)---the relative

\subsection{Motifs and Function Boundaries}

Motif discovery across $k{=}4{-}12$ identifies $7{,}623$ unique patterns
meeting frequency/coverage thresholds; the top-100 per length yield
$798$ motifs.  High-scoring motifs fall into three categories:
function epilogues (top-ranked 4-mer
\texttt{pop pop pop ret} appears in $27.9\%$ of functions),
call sequences (\texttt{call} preceded by argument-loading
\texttt{lea}/\texttt{mov}), and prologues
(\texttt{endbr64; push; mov}, reflecting CET + ABI conventions).
Position-0 of functions is dominated by \texttt{endbr64}
($p{\approx}0.986$, system-dependent on CET); positions 1--2 are
\texttt{push} ($0.672$) and \texttt{mov} ($0.671$),
ABI-mandated.  Boundary entropy at position 0 is $0.12$\,bits
(vs.\ corpus unigram entropy $3.99$); 3 conserved start positions and
0 conserved end positions classify the corpus as loosely structured.

\subsection{Similarity and Manifold}

The smoke-corpus NCD matrix (\zlib{}) ranges $0.956$--$1.000$ (mean
$0.991$); under \lzma{} it ranges $0.852$--$0.993$ (mean $0.967$).
Closest pairs are intra-category compression utilities
(\texttt{bzip2}--\texttt{xz}: $0.956$).
A one-sided Mann--Whitney U test confirms within-category pairs are
stochastically closer than between
(\zlib{}: $p{=}0.013$, permutation $p{<}0.001$, $r{=}0.600$;
\lzma{}: $p{=}0.005$, permutation $p{=}0.003$, $r{=}0.698$).
The absolute gap is small (mean within $0.972$ vs.\ between $0.992$
under \zlib{}: $13.8\%$ of the between-group baseline), so we interpret
NCD as a weak but significant continuous signal, not a basis for hard
clustering.

N-gram cosine similarity over L1-normalised TF vectors decreases
with $n$: $0.953$ (bigrams), $0.897$ (trigrams), $0.812$ (4-grams),
with standard deviations $0.037/0.080/0.132$.  The growing spread is
consistent with rare $n$-grams becoming discriminative as $n$ grows.
The UMAP projection of the NCD-\zlib{} matrix
(Fig.~\ref{fig:umap}) places small compression utilities in a region
distinct from large interactive programs (\texttt{bash},
\texttt{vim}, \texttt{git}, \texttt{python3}).

\textbf{Manifold dimensionality.}
Even at $n{=}2$, only $6.7\%$ of possible opcode pairs are observed
($5{,}757$ of $85{,}849$); coverage drops to $0.15\%$ at $n{=}3$.
N-gram space coverage drops sharply with $n$ (Table~\ref{tab:coverage}):
even bigrams cover only $6.7\%$ of the theoretical maximum; trigrams
$0.15\%$; 4-grams ${\sim}10^{-5}$.
Corpus-level PCA on top-200 bigram vectors finds $d_{95}{=}8$ of $200$
($4\%$)---a thin structured manifold.

$I(1){\approx}1.12$\,bits, far above shuffled $0.086$; MI half-life is

lag $50$, $I(50){=}0.232$ vs.\ shuffled $0.086$, suggesting weak but
non-zero long-range dependencies consistent with repetitive call/ret
patterns.

\subsection{Clone Detection}

LSH yielded $556$ unique candidate pairs on the $6{,}025$ functions of
the smoke corpus; Smith--Waterman alignment confirmed $537$ ($96.6\%$).
These form $117$ families, four of which span multiple binaries.
Type 1 (exact, $J{\geq}0.95$) dominates ($63.7\%$), consistent with
verbatim compiler/linker boilerplate (function prologues,
\texttt{\_\_libc\_start\_main} wrappers).  $5.8\%$ of all functions are
in some clone family; the largest family (size 14) is the standard ABI
prologue.  Stub-exclusion analysis (below) shows $46\%$ of functions
are toolchain-generated, so cross-binary clones include both
toolchain-inserted and programmer-authored patterns---the latter a
smaller subset.  Per-binary clone density varies from $9.6\%$
(\texttt{bash}, reflecting its inline library of shell built-in helpers)
and $5.0\%$ (\texttt{sed}) down to ${<}0.5\%$ for \texttt{git} and
\texttt{vim}, suggesting hand-crafted code with unique control flow
is less prone to accidental near-duplication.

\section{Validation and Robustness}
\label{sec:validation}

We conducted a systematic validation programme targeting four threats:
extraction errors, boilerplate confounds, corpus dependence, and
compiler dependence; full scripts and intermediate data are in
\texttt{validation/}.

\textbf{Extraction.}
An independent reference parser was applied to five binaries
(\texttt{grep}, \texttt{bash}, \texttt{find}, \texttt{tar},
\texttt{xz}); per-mnemonic counts matched exactly
(e.g.\ \texttt{bash}: $241{,}789$ instructions).
Hard-link deduplication was verified against 7 inode-sharing groups;
timeout handling was confirmed via mock injection.

\textbf{Synthetic and bootstrap checks.}
Five synthetic corpus families (uniform, Zipf-shuffled, order-2 Markov,
templated, mixed) passed all 26 metric checks.  Binary-level bootstrap
($N{=}200$) gives MLE $\hat{\alpha}{=}1.44\,[1.36,1.53]$,
$\hr{5}{=}2.86\,[2.79,2.94]$\,bits, mean \zlib{} ratio
$0.22\,[0.18,0.27]$; all CIs exclude reversal.  Leave-one-binary-out
identifies no influential outlier ($\hat{\alpha}$ varies ${\leq}2\%$).

\textbf{Boilerplate ablation.}
Sixteen ablated variants (strip first/last $k{\in}\{1,2,3,5\}$ instructions,
remove \texttt{endbr64}, strip prologues/epilogues, full strip) preserve
all metrics within $3.2\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}).  Toolchain
functions (startup + stubs) account for $46\%$ of functions but only
$0.6\%$ of instructions; removing them changes mean \zlib{} by
$-0.006$ and $\hr{5}$ by $+0.003$.  The findings are intrinsic to
application code, not boilerplate.  Operand-aware variants
(opcode$+$register class, opcode$+$immediate bucket) confirm the
entropy gap persists.

\textbf{External validation.}
Three disjoint corpora pass all pre-registered consistency criteria
(Table~\ref{tab:corpora}): real entropy below shuffled, real compression
below shuffled, cross-corpus bigram LM perplexity within $1.2\times$ of
within-corpus.  An expanded matrix of 100 binaries
($\{\mathtt{gcc},\mathtt{clang}\}\times\{$\texttt{-O0,-O1,-O2,-O3,-Os}$\}\times 10$
programs) gives one-way ANOVA $R^2$: program identity dominates
($R^2{=}0.40{-}0.66$), compiler identity ${<}3\%$ for all four metrics
(Zipf $\alpha$, $H_1$, $\hr{2}$, \zlib{} ratio), confirming
compiler-invariance.  Scaling from the 15-binary smoke corpus to the
40-binary coreutils-system superset
(Table~\ref{tab:scaling}) shows MLE $\hat{\alpha}$, entropy rates,
and compression ratios are nearly identical, with manifold
dimensionality growing modestly ($d_{95}{:}8{\to}14$) as expected
when category breadth increases.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Compiled programs do not explore opcode-sequence space uniformly.  They
are confined to a low-dimensional, highly structured manifold shaped by
ABI conventions (CET + frame setup), compiler optimisation idioms,
algorithmic patterns, and clone reuse (linker-inserted stubs and shared
libc helpers).  Concentrated vocabulary
(MLE $\hat{\alpha}{\approx}1.4$), rapidly decaying entropy rates, very
high compressibility relative to baselines, and $d_{95}{\approx}8$
collectively show programs are extraordinarily predictable at the
instruction level.  The validation results
(Section~\ref{sec:validation}) demonstrate this is not a boilerplate
artefact: stripping changes metrics by ${\leq}3.2\%$, findings replicate
across three corpora, and compiler identity explains ${<}3\%$ of variance.

\textbf{Implications for LMs on code.}
The naturalness hypothesis~\cite{hindle2012naturalness} attributes LLM
success on source code to its statistical regularity.  Our results
extend this to the binary level: an LM faces a small effective
vocabulary, highly non-uniform transitions, MI decay within ${\approx}2$
steps, and low intrinsic dimensionality.  The bigram LM
(Section~\ref{sec:lm}) achieves PPL $8.5$ within $0.07$\,bits of the
conditional-entropy bound---simple count statistics already capture
nearly all bigram-order structure.

\textbf{Threats to validity.}
Construct: compression conflates frequency skew with sequential
redundancy, mitigated but not eliminated by the shuffled baseline.
Internal: the boilerplate-driven hypothesis is rejected
(Section~\ref{sec:validation}); operand-aware variants confirm the
entropy gap.
External: all binaries are x86-64 ELF on GNU/Linux; ARM64, RISC-V,
WASM, MSVC, and other source languages are untested.
Statistical: $N{=}15$ is a convenience sample; tokens within a binary
are non-independent, so corpus-level claims effectively have $N{=}15$
observations, not millions.

% =============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Across a 15-binary smoke corpus (2.91\,M instructions), three
independent validation corpora, and a 100-binary compiler matrix,
opcode frequencies are heavily concentrated
(MLE $\hat{\alpha}{\approx}1.4$), entropy rates decrease by
$1.12$\,bits from $n{=}1$ to $n{=}5$ vs.\ $0.30$ shuffled, real
sequences compress to $22\%$ vs.\ $36\%$ shuffled and $67\%$ random,
$7{,}623$ unique motifs capture compiler idioms, the manifold
occupies $d_{95}{=}8$ of $200$ bigram dimensions, and a bigram LM
attains PPL $8.5$ within $0.07$\,bits of the lower bound.
Eight of ten empirical claims are strong, one moderate, one
unsupported.  The thin-manifold hypothesis explains why language
models and similarity search are effective on binary code.

\textbf{Future work.}
(i) multi-ISA replication (ARM64, RISC-V, WASM, JVM bytecode);
(ii) operand-level entropy and manifold analysis;
(iii) compiler / source-language fingerprinting;
(iv) Transformer-LM evaluation vs.\ the $n$-gram baseline;
(v) clone-guided vulnerability propagation across binaries sharing
toolchain or application clones.
